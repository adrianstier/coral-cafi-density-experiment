Corallivorous Snail Analysis

Author: MRB CAFI Analysis Date: November 20, 2025

Overview

This analysis examines corallivorous and coral-associated snail (gastropod) patterns in response to experimental coral density manipulation. We address the reviewer comment regarding the notable hyperaggregation of snails on multi-coral reefs.

Key Questions

  1. How did snail abundance shift with coral number?
  2. Were snail distributions clustered (hyperaggregated) on 6-coral reefs?
  3. How were snails correlated with coral performance?

3. Hyperaggregation Pattern

To assess whether snails showed hyperaggregation (stronger-than-expected increase with coral number), we compare the fold-increase from 1-coral to 6-coral treatments.

Expected under proportional scaling: 6× increase (6 corals vs 1 coral)

Observed: - Mean snails on 1-coral reefs: 21.50 - Mean snails on 6-coral reefs: 27.07 - Fold increase: 1.26×

Interpretation: This represents hypoaggregation - snails increased less than proportionally with coral number. However, this pattern masks the significant increase in species richness, suggesting that while total abundance doesn’t scale with coral number, the diversity of snail species does.

Summary and Interpretation

Key Findings

  1. Snail total abundance did not change significantly with coral number (F = 0.43, p = 0.660)

  2. Snail species richness increased significantly with coral number (F = 3.73, p = 0.032)

    • 6-coral reefs had ~37% more snail species than 1-coral reefs

  3. No hyperaggregation in total abundance: Snails showed only a 1.3-fold increase from 1-coral to 6-coral treatments, well below the 6× expected under proportional scaling.

  4. No relationship with coral performance: Snail abundance was not significantly correlated with coral condition (r = -0.076, p = 0.625)

  5. One species showed significant treatment effects: Morula zebrina was significantly more abundant on 6-coral reefs (p = 0.031)

Biological Interpretation

The lack of relationship between snail abundance and coral performance suggests these gastropods may have neutral effects on coral hosts, or that positive and negative effects cancel out across species. Importantly:

  1. Species richness increases but not abundance - This suggests multi-coral reefs provide habitat for more snail species but don’t support higher densities per species.

  2. No evidence for snail-mediated coral decline - The non-significant correlation between snail abundance and coral performance (PC1) does not support the hypothesis that gastropods are driving the observed density-dependent decline in coral condition.

  3. Individual species patterns are weak - Only 1 of 7 tested species showed significant treatment effects, and none showed significant correlations with coral performance.

Conclusions

Contrary to expectations, snails did NOT show strong hyperaggregation on multi-coral reefs. While species richness was significantly higher on 6-coral reefs (p = 0.032), total snail abundance did not differ among treatments (p = 0.660).

Snails are unlikely to be the primary driver of density-dependent coral decline. The lack of correlation between snail abundance and coral performance (r = -0.076, p = 0.625) suggests that gastropods are not responsible for the observed reduction in coral condition at high densities.

These results indicate that while Joe’s observation about snail diversity on multi-coral reefs is supported, the hypothesis that corallivorous snails mediate density-dependent coral decline is not supported by these data.
